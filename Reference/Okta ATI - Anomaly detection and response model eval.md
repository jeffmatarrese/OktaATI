## Pre-evaluation Write-up

### 0.0 Overview

The Agentic Threat Intelligence module uses an LLM-powered pipeline to monitor AI agent behavior across applications and surface actionable intelligence to SOC analysts. The model performs four distinct functions: (1) classifying agent behavior as anomalous or normal, (2) correlating activity across applications to identify coordinated or escalating threats, (3) recommending an enforcement action (stall, scope restriction, or session kill) calibrated to threat severity and confidence, and (4) generating natural language explanations of why an agent was flagged. Each function carries a different risk profile. Misclassification wastes analyst time or misses threats, a bad recommendation could disrupt production or leave a threat uncontained, and a hallucinated explanation could lead an analyst to take the wrong action based on fabricated reasoning.

### 1.1 Eval Set Design (The Golden Dataset)

**Size and composition:** 500 labeled scenarios split across four categories: true anomalies (30%), benign activity that resembles anomalies (30%), edge cases requiring org-specific context (25%), and adversarial inputs designed to fool the model (15%). The dataset skews toward hard cases because easy classifications don't stress-test the model.

**Prompt selection methodology:** Scenarios are constructed from three sources. First, real anonymized agent activity logs from Okta's early-access customers. Second, synthetic scenarios generated by security engineers representing known attack patterns and their benign equivalents. Third, adversarial prompts designed to test failure modes, mimicking normal behavior and acting slowly, displaying anomalous behavior in one context but normal in another, or intentionally trying to sabotage the model's scoring mechanism.

**Ground truth definition and SME panel:** Ground truth is established by a standing panel of 20 security SMEs with 10+ years of enterprise SOC experience and demonstrated expertise in identity-based threats and emerging AI security vectors. Each scenario is independently labeled by 4+ panelists across three dimensions: binary anomaly classification, recommended enforcement tier with justification, and a reference explanation describing what the analyst should see. Consensus is determined by majority agreement. Inter-reviewer agreement is tracked as a meta-metric; if agreement drops below 80%, the labeling rubric needs refinement before the eval is valid.

The 20-person panel is large enough to provide 3-4 independent reviews per scenario with robust consensus data, while small enough to maintain quality control. At an estimated cost of $120K-160K per labeling round, this is a fraction of the reputational and financial cost of launching with unreliable ground truth, particularly for a company still rebuilding trust after the 2023 breach.

**Two-round structure:** Round 1 establishes the golden dataset: all 20 SMEs label the initial 500 scenarios, majority consensus sets ground truth, and the results train the model and calibrate the LLM judge. Round 2 occurs after training: the same panel reviews a sample of the judge's graded outputs to validate that the automated evaluation pipeline produces reliable scores.

**Standing retention:** The panel is retained on an ongoing basis. When the golden dataset is refreshed quarterly with new real-world scenarios (see 1.7), the same SMEs label new cases with an established understanding of the rubric. This continuity prevents labeling drift across eval cycles and ensures ground truth evolves consistently as agent threat patterns change.

### 1.2 Technical Performance Metrics

**Hallucination Rate:** Measured on the explanation generation function (function 4). A hallucination is the model citing a behavioral signal, application, or data point not present in the input activity log. Detected by programmatic comparison between the explanation and source data. Target: below 1%.

**Correctness/Accuracy:** Anomaly classification is evaluated via precision and recall, with recall weighted more heavily because a missed threat is more costly than a false alarm. Recommended action accuracy is graded by SMEs on a 3-point scale: correct, defensible but suboptimal, or wrong. Cross-app correlation accuracy measures whether the model identifies related activity across applications without fabricating unsupported connections.

**Latency:** Detection pipeline (agent action to anomaly flag): P95 < 2 seconds, P99 < 5 seconds. Dashboard rendering (analyst query to result): P95 < 500ms, P99 < 1 second.

**Cost:** Cost-per-inference must be benchmarked against SOC analyst-hour cost to ensure the feature is unit-economically viable at scale, with a two-model architecture (lightweight classifier for routine activity, frontier model for edge cases) as the primary cost lever.

### 1.3 Business KPI Alignment

The technical metrics connect to business outcomes through a chain. Classification accuracy drives false positive rates, which affect analyst trust. When analyst trust is compromised, MTTIC (the primary A/B experiment metric) goes up, hurting both adoption and behavioral data that feeds the model. That last link is critical as the data flywheel underpins Okta's competitive moat.

### 1.4 Evaluation Strategy: Human-in-the-Loop before Agent-as-a-Judge

**Stage 1 — Human SME Evals.** The 20-person panel grades model outputs on the full 500-scenario golden dataset across all three dimensions. This is expensive ($120K-160K per round) but irreplaceable for a domain where "correct" is context-dependent. An automated system cannot determine whether "restrict scope" is the right call vs. "stall and escalate" without understanding operational implications that vary by industry, compliance regime, and organizational risk tolerance. Human evaluation also surfaces unanticipated failure modes that feed back into the golden dataset.

**Stage 2 — Machine evals at scale.** Once SMEs establish the grading rubric and validated scored examples, a frontier-class LLM evaluates the model against thousands of synthetic variations generated by mutating the golden dataset to represent a broad variety of theoretical scenarios. The judge grades each output using SME-scored examples as few-shot references, scaling from 500 to 10,000+ scenarios at a fraction of the cost.

**Calibration check:** A random 5% sample of judge-graded outputs is reviewed by the SME panel each cycle. If SME-judge agreement drops below 85%, the judge prompt and rubric are revised before results are treated as valid.

### 1.5 Success and Guardrail Metrics

**Main Objective:** Greater than 98% classification accuracy on the golden dataset, with recall above 99%. Recommended action accuracy must reach 90%+ "correct" from SMEs, with less than 2% rated "wrong action."

**Guardrails:** Hallucination rate below 1%. Detection pipeline P95 under 2 seconds. Dashboard P95 under 500ms. The model must handle 100% of adversarial scenarios without producing a "wrong action" recommendation.

These metrics serve as critical gates. If the model performance is not at or above this criteria threshold, more training and tuning is required before proceeding to the A/B test.

### 1.6 Reliability and Safety Guardrails

**Input validation:** Malformed or incomplete activity logs are flagged and routed to manual review rather than processed with missing context. **Output filtering:** Every model-generated explanation is programmatically checked against source data before reaching the analyst; unverifiable claims are stripped and replaced with a verification flag. **Confidence gating:** Below a defined confidence threshold, detections route to manual review regardless of classification, preventing low-confidence automated enforcement. **Adversarial defense:** The eval includes prompt injection attempts embedded in agent metadata or application responses, testing whether the model can be fooled by agents that craft activity to appear benign.

### 1.7 Risks and Potential Impact

**Model rot.** Agent behavior patterns will evolve as LLMs mature and attack methods adapt. To mitigate this, the golden dataset is refreshed quarterly with new scenarios labeled by the standing SME panel, and the LLM judge runs weekly against the latest model version to detect drift before it reaches production.

**False confidence in automation.** Strong eval results could lead to reduced SOC oversight. The standing SME panel and 5% calibration check each cycle are structural safeguards ensuring human expertise remains engaged as a permanent feature of the program, not an afterthought.

**Cost scaling.** Continuous monitoring across thousands of agents per tenant could erode margin if the model is invoked on every action rather than pre-filtered suspicious activity. Cost-per-tenant must be tracked as a business metric alongside technical performance, and may inform the ultimate pricing structure (per action rather than per seat/identity).

**Ground truth fragility.** The entire framework depends on SME-labeled ground truth. If the rubric is ambiguous, if SMEs disagree frequently, or if scenarios don't represent real-world distributions, the eval produces misleading confidence. Inter-reviewer agreement must be above 80%. If it drops, the eval needs fixing before model performance can be meaningfully assessed. The standing panel and two-round structure minimize this risk, but it remains the single most important failure mode to monitor.
