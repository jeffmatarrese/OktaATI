Press Release
Okta wants to be the reason your company stops blocking AI agents The Verge · May 2026
Marcus O'Brien had a problem he couldn't see. As CISO at a mid-market SaaS company running infrastructure across AWS, GCP, and Azure, he was responsible for convincing his board that the company's 400-agent AI footprint was under control. It wasn't. His team had only catalogued about 63% of their deployed agents. The rest were shadow workflows stood up by DevOps, marketing, and IT without security review.
The breaking point came during a SOC standup, when analysts flagged a permission escalation and a spike in API calls from an AWS workflow. O'Brien asked the two questions that matter: what's the blast radius, and whose identity is this agent operating under? Nobody could answer. The culprit was a marketing automation tied to a shared service account, moving sensitive data to an unapproved endpoint. "An agent without an identity is a contractor wandering your building with no badge," O'Brien said. "We killed the entire workflow just to be safe."
He's not alone in that dilemma. Security teams across the enterprise face a losing binary: let AI agents run ungoverned and accept shadow IT risk, or block them entirely and fall behind. Non-human identities now outnumber human ones 144 to 1, and 70% of knowledge workers are bringing their own AI tools to work regardless of policy. Most security teams have no way to answer three basic questions: which agents exist, what they can access, and what they're actually doing.
Okta is launching Agentic Threat Intelligence (ATI) to close that gap. The product gives security teams real-time visibility and proportionate response capabilities across every AI agent touching their environment. It connects to agent platforms including Anthropic, Google Vertex, Salesforce Agentforce, and Microsoft Copilot Studio through Okta's 8,200+ app integration network. When an agent is registered or discovered running unregistered, Okta monitors its authorization requests, data access, and cross-application behavior. If an agent starts behaving anomalously, the system assigns a risk score and recommends an action: monitor, throttle, restrict scope, or terminate the session.
The critical differentiator is the data underneath. Because Okta brokers authorization between agents and applications, it sees behavioral patterns across its entire customer base. The anomaly detection models train on that aggregate, anonymized dataset, so a threat pattern identified at one company improves detection for every Okta customer. More agents monitored means better models, fewer false positives, and more trust in the system.
Dani Alvarez, a Tier 2 SOC analyst managing 1,200 agents at a financial services firm, described the current reality: "By the time I manually assemble cross-app context to understand what happened, the agent has already finished its action. I'm doing forensics, not prevention." What she needs is a way to freeze a specific flagged action for review, not a kill switch that takes down an entire workflow. ATI's tiered enforcement is designed for exactly that.
Within six weeks of piloting ATI, O'Brien's team closed the visibility gap on their agent fleet, surfaced 14 overprivileged agents, and caught three accessing data outside their approved scope. "Before, we would have killed the entire workflow. Now we freeze the specific action, investigate, and let the business keep moving."
That capability changed the conversation with his board entirely. "My board doesn't care about a vendor logo. They want the answer to one question: what is our AI risk exposure?" O'Brien said. "The headline I put up isn't 'Implemented Agentic Threat Intelligence.' It's 'AI Innovation Safely Enabled: 100% of Agent Fleet Governed.' I tie our security controls directly back to revenue protection, operational resilience, and regulatory compliance. That's the language that gets a board to say yes to more AI investment, not less."
O'Brien has since gone from blocking AI to enabling it. "My job used to be saying no," he said. "Now it's making yes possible safely. That's a much better job."
Frequently Asked Questions
External
How is ATI different from our existing SIEM or XDR? SIEM and XDR monitor infrastructure events: network traffic, endpoint behavior, log anomalies. ATI monitors the identity layer: which agent performed which action across which applications, and whether that behavior is consistent with the agent's registered purpose. A SIEM might flag unusual API traffic. Okta can tell you it came from a specific agent, provisioned by a specific team, accessing resources outside its approved scope, exhibiting a pattern flagged as malicious across other Okta customers last week. The two systems are complementary, not competitive. ATI feeds enriched identity context into your existing SIEM, eliminating the manual context assembly that currently takes analysts 20+ minutes per alert.
What happens when the system flags an agent? Does it shut things down automatically? By default, no. The system surfaces the alert, risk score, behavioral evidence, and a recommended response. Security teams decide. Organizations can configure auto-enforcement for deterministic policy violations (e.g., an agent accessing a resource outside its allow-list), but the default is human-in-the-loop triage. O'Brien: "I'm much more willing to automate a scope restriction than a full session kill." Alvarez described why proportionate enforcement changes the dynamic with engineering: "Instead of 'I killed your app, prove it was safe,' the conversation becomes 'I paused this specific call because it didn't match baseline, can you confirm?'" We believe teams should build trust with the system before enabling full automation.
How does Okta handle data privacy if the models train on cross-customer data? The models train on anonymized, aggregate behavioral patterns, not raw customer data. The training set consists of abstracted signatures: "an agent of type X accessing resource category Y at frequency Z is anomalous." Individual agent identities, application names, and user data are never shared across customers. This is consistent with how Okta's existing Identity Threat Protection processes cross-customer signals today.
Which agent platforms are supported at launch? Anthropic (Claude), Google Vertex AI, Salesforce Agentforce, Microsoft Copilot Studio, Amazon Bedrock, ServiceNow AI Platform, and custom agents using the Cross App Access (XAA) protocol. Integrations for DataRobot, Boomi, Glean, and Workday are coming in subsequent quarters. XAA is open source, so any platform can implement native Okta governance without waiting for a bespoke integration.
Does this require agents to be pre-registered with Okta? No. The system operates in two modes: governed agents that are registered with identity policies, and discovered agents that Okta detects operating in the environment without formal registration. Agent Discovery, part of Okta's Identity Security Posture Management (ISPM), runs continuously to surface shadow agents, map their potential blast radius, and flag them for IT review. Discovered agents can then be retroactively registered or blocked. This matters because, as O'Brien described, teams across the business stood up agents before any oversight framework existed, and manual log-scraping is not a scalable answer.
What compliance and audit capabilities does this provide? Every agent action, detection, triage decision, and enforcement action is logged with full attribution in a tamper-evident audit trail, structured for SOC 2, ISO 27001, HIPAA, and financial services frameworks (SEC, FINRA, OCC). O'Brien framed the bar: "My auditors won't accept a dashboard screenshot. They need verifiable actor attribution, data lineage, and an explainable reasoning path." ATI is designed to be regulator-ready out of the box.
What's the detection latency? Detection operates in near-real-time on the authorization event stream. When an agent makes an authorization request through Okta, the system evaluates it against the agent's behavioral baseline and cross-customer pattern library before the session completes. P50 detection latency is under 500ms; P95 is under 2 seconds. For auto-enforcement policies, the action executes inline with the authorization flow. But as Alvarez noted, detection speed alone isn't the value: "If a vendor detects threats 40% faster but doubles my alert volume, I'm worse off. I need a decision engine, not an alert cannon."
Internal
Why build this as a new product instead of extending Identity Threat Protection? ITP was built for human identity threats: credential theft, session hijacking, phishing. Agent threats are structurally different. Agents don't have passwords to steal. They don't get phished. An agent making 500 API calls per hour might be normal; a human doing that is a red flag. The detection models, risk scoring, and response actions all need to be purpose-built for non-human behavioral patterns. Retrofitting ITP would mean forcing human-centric assumptions onto a fundamentally different problem space.
Microsoft already launched Entra Agent ID. How does Okta compete? Entra Agent ID went GA in May 2026 as part of Agent 365, bundled into M365 E7 at $99/user/month. Microsoft now claims cross-platform support for non-Microsoft agents via their Auth SDK sidecar and workload identity federation, including AWS Bedrock and n8n. This is a meaningful move, and we should not underestimate it. But the limitations are structural. Third-party agents face hard caps (250 agent identity blueprints per tenant for non-Microsoft platforms using app-only permissions). More critically, once an agent calls out beyond the Microsoft ecosystem into other SaaS platforms, cloud providers, or internal APIs, visibility degrades significantly. As one independent analysis put it, "Agent 365 gives you a strong view of activity in and around Microsoft 365, but mapping usage all the way out into the rest of your hybrid environment still takes additional tooling." Entra gives you strong governance for agents operating within M365 and Azure. Okta gives you the cross-platform behavioral picture.
The trust argument also cuts in our favor here. The Entra Agent ID Administrator role suffered a critical privilege escalation vulnerability in early 2026. Silverfort researchers demonstrated that the role could take over arbitrary service principals, enabling full tenant takeover, and found the conditions for this escalation path existed in 99% of tenants analyzed. Microsoft patched it in April, but for CISOs evaluating agent governance platforms, this reinforces the risk of single-vendor dependency for the most sensitive layer of the stack.
O'Brien named cross-cloud compatibility as a deal-killer: "If a vendor can't prove seamless visibility across all three of my clouds, the deal is dead." Okta's positioning as the vendor-neutral layer is strengthened, not threatened, by Entra's expansion. Notably, Okta now supports organizations running Entra or Ping as their workforce identity provider, layering agent governance on top without requiring a rip-and-replace. The pitch isn't "Okta instead of Entra." It's "Okta on top of whatever you already run."
How do you handle the cold start problem? Early customers get value from two sources before the cross-customer models mature. First, rule-based detections work from day one: scope violations, unauthorized access, and permission anomalies are deterministic checks that don't need ML. O'Brien was clear on this: "I'm not handing over the kill switch based on a confidence score. The agent must violate a strict policy boundary." Second, Okta's existing ITP already processes billions of identity events. Those behavioral baselines for application access provide a meaningful starting point for agent anomaly detection. The ML models improve the system over time, but the product is useful without them.
What about false positives? This is the hardest problem in security tooling and the primary reason we're investing in the cross-customer behavioral dataset. Alvarez: "If this tool generates false positives at scale, my analysts drown in alerts. We don't gain visibility, we lose it." Our approach: ship with conservative default thresholds, let customers tune sensitivity based on risk tolerance, and continuously improve models as the aggregate dataset grows. Targets: FPR below 2% for stall-and-escalate actions and below 0.1% for automated enforcement within the first year of GA.
What's the biggest risk? Trust. Okta is asking enterprises to route their most sensitive AI governance decisions through a platform that suffered a significant security breach in 2023. The product strategy is sound, but execution must be flawless. Any incident involving ATI infrastructure would confirm the market's worst fears. This is why we launch with human-in-the-loop defaults and build toward automation gradually. We need to earn trust before we ask for it.
What's the growth model? Three layers. Land: sell ATI as an add-on to existing Workforce Identity enterprise customers through the same CISO relationship. Expand: as customers deploy more agents, usage-based pricing grows revenue without additional sales effort. Flywheel: every customer's agent telemetry improves the detection models, which reduces false positives, which makes the product more compelling for the next customer. Growth lever: offering a free 30-day agent discovery scan (inventory only, no detection) that shows CISOs how many ungoverned agents are already operating. When O'Brien's team ran theirs, they found 37% of their agent fleet was undocumented. Showing prospects their own shadow agent count is the conversion moment.
What are we intentionally not building in v1? Automated agent provisioning or lifecycle management (separate product motion, adds complexity that distracts from the monitoring value prop). Integration with non-Okta identity providers for the core detection engine (though Okta for AI Agents now supports non-Okta IdPs for agent governance broadly). Predictive threat modeling (v1 is reactive/detective, not predictive). Multi-tenant management for MSPs and MSSPs. Each is a plausible v2 feature, but shipping any of them in v1 would delay launch and dilute the core promise: see your agents, understand the risks, act fast.
Why launch now instead of waiting for the market to mature? The data flywheel advantage compounds over time. Every month of agent behavioral data Okta collects that competitors don't is a month of model training they can't replicate. If we wait for the market to consolidate around standards and platforms, we lose the first-mover advantage on the dataset that makes this product defensible. The market for agent governance is forming right now. Enterprises are making buy decisions in 2026 that will lock in for years. Microsoft is already shipping Entra Agent ID. Startups like Oasis Security are carving out the hybrid governance layer. Being early with a useful product beats being late with a perfect one. The discovery scan alone (showing CISOs their own ungoverned agent count) creates urgency that didn't exist six months ago.
What are the success metrics? Primary: 25% of Workforce Identity enterprise customers (5,000+ employee orgs) activate ATI within 12 months of GA. Secondary: median time from detection to containment under 5 minutes (50% reduction vs. manual workflows); NPS among security operations users above 40 within two quarters; false positive rate below 2% on stall-and-escalate actions by month 6. Guardrails: platform latency increase under 50ms at P95 for standard auth flows; false-positive-related support tickets below 3% of active ATI deployments per month.
Why an ML classifier instead of an LLM for the core detection engine? The production detector is a purpose-built ML classifier trained on Okta's proprietary behavioral data, not an LLM. We validated this empirically during prototyping. A bare-metal LLM used as a stand-in classifier produced zero false positives but only 33% recall on ambiguous cases, exactly the "this isn't obviously bad but you should pause it" scenarios where proportionate enforcement matters most. The feature-based ML classifier lifted recall on those cases to 73% while keeping inference latency at sub-millisecond. The ML model also produces probabilities rather than natural-language explanations, which matters for the audit trail. Production pairs the classifier with a separate SHAP-based explanation pipeline to surface the underlying signals analysts need to trust and verify the output.
What engineering investment is required? Core team is approximately 15-20 engineers across three workstreams: the agent telemetry pipeline (ingestion, normalization, storage of cross-platform behavioral data), the detection engine (ML models, rule evaluation, risk scoring), and the product surface (dashboard, alert workflow, triage UX, SIEM integrations). The telemetry pipeline leverages existing Okta infrastructure for identity event processing. The detection engine is the primary new investment and requires ML engineering talent with security domain expertise. Estimated time to GA from project kickoff is 9-12 months, with a limited beta at month 6 targeting 20-30 design partners.
What happens if XAA adoption stalls? XAA makes cross-platform governance scalable, but ATI doesn't require universal adoption. Agents on platforms with existing OIN integrations (Salesforce, Vertex, Bedrock) are monitored through those integrations today. XAA expands the addressable surface and accelerates the data flywheel. If adoption is slower than projected, the product still works across fewer platforms and models train on a narrower dataset. The risk is slower compounding of the data advantage, not product failure.
How should sales position ATI against competitive objections? The most common objection will be "Microsoft already includes this in our E7 license." Sales should reframe: Entra Agent ID is strong for Microsoft-native agents, and ATI isn't asking customers to replace it. ATI is the cross-platform layer that Entra can't provide. The second objection will be pricing. Per-agent pricing creates buyer skepticism because auto-discovery looks like a budget Trojan horse. We're piloting a platform-tier pricing model alongside per-agent for enterprise accounts to address this, charging a flat premium on existing identity contracts rather than scaling linearly with agent count. Lead with the free 30-day discovery scan to build urgency before the pricing conversation.
Does ATI cannibalize existing Okta products? No. ATI is additive to the Workforce Identity platform. It requires Workforce Identity as a prerequisite (agents must authenticate through Okta for the system to monitor them), so it reinforces rather than displaces the core platform. There is potential overlap with Identity Threat Protection in edge cases where an agent's behavior triggers both human-identity and agent-identity detection rules, but the products are architecturally distinct and target different threat models. ATI increases the strategic value of the Workforce Identity platform by giving customers a new reason to consolidate agent governance through Okta rather than adopting point solutions.
What UX principles guide the SOC analyst dashboard? The dashboard is designed for the analyst who gets the alert at 3 AM, not the executive reviewing a monthly report. Every detection surfaces pre-assembled cross-app context so the analyst doesn't manually reconstruct the timeline. Risk scores are accompanied by the specific signals that produced them, because analysts won't trust a black-box output. Enforcement actions are tiered and reversible by default: monitor, throttle, restrict scope, terminate. The UX goal is reducing mean time from alert to informed decision. Alvarez's benchmark: "I need to go from 30 minutes to under 15 for investigation and containment."
How does this affect Okta's business model? ATI is a premium add-on priced per monitored agent, creating a usage-based revenue stream that scales with AI adoption. It deepens platform stickiness: once security workflows depend on Okta's behavioral models and cross-app visibility, switching costs are substantial. The feature targets the same buyer (CISO) but shifts the conversation from "access management cost" to "AI enablement investment," changing procurement dynamics and budget allocation.
What's the go-to-market strategy? Three motions. First, existing enterprise accounts: ATI is a natural upsell in quarterly business reviews, especially for customers who've expressed concern about AI governance. Second, design partner program: 20-30 beta customers get early access in exchange for feedback, case studies, and co-development of detection rules for their industry verticals. These become reference customers for GA. Third, category creation: Okta needs to establish "Agentic Threat Intelligence" as a recognized product category through analyst briefings (Gartner, Forrester), conference keynotes (RSA, Oktane), and thought leadership. The goal is to be the defining vendor before competitors frame the category on their terms.
